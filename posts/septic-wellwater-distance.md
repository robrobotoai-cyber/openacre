# Septic Systems & Well Water: Safe Distances & What Can Go Wrong

If you have both a septic system and a well on your property, **their proximity matters**. A lot.

A failing septic system can contaminate your well—making water undrinkable and creating a serious health hazard. Here's what you need to know.

## Safe Distances (Washington State Rules)

Washington requires **minimum distances** between septic drain fields and wells:

| Component | Minimum Distance | Why |
|-----------|-----------------|-----|
| Septic tank | 50 feet | Prevent gross contamination |
| Drain field | 100 feet | Filter pathogens through soil |
| Pump tank (aerobic) | 50 feet | Active system element |
| Well | Varies | Depends on soil type |

**But here's the catch:** These are **minimums**. They assume:
- Perfect soil conditions
- System operating properly
- No failure or unusual circumstances

**In real life:** 100 feet isn't always safe if soil drains poorly or the system is failing.

---

## How Septic Contaminates Wells

### Path 1: Direct Groundwater Flow
- Septic effluent percolates into groundwater
- If well is downhill/downgradient, contamination follows
- Poor soil = faster contamination
- **Timeline:** Days to weeks

### Path 2: Through Aquifer
- Water moves through underground layers
- Can travel 100+ feet if aquifer is connected
- Often invisible until detected in testing
- **Timeline:** Weeks to months

### Path 3: System Failure
- Cracked tank leaks raw sewage directly into ground
- Drain field saturated (not absorbing)
- No filtration = direct contamination
- **Timeline:** Immediate

## Signs Your Well Is Contaminated

🚨 **Visual:**
- Discolored water (brown, grey, cloudy)
- Sediment/particles in water
- Visible biofilm buildup

🚨 **Smell:**
- Sewage or septic odor
- Rotten egg smell (hydrogen sulfide)
- Musty, earthy smell

🚨 **Taste:**
- Salty or bitter taste
- Chemical taste (rare but possible)

🚨 **Health:**
- Gastroenteritis (E. coli, cryptosporidium)
- Skin rashes or infections
- Headaches after using water

## Testing Your Well

**Get a test if:**
- You notice water quality changes
- Your septic system recently failed
- You're buying a property with both
- You live near a neighbor's failing system

**Test for:**
- Bacteria (E. coli, coliform)
- Nitrates (septic byproduct)
- Viruses
- Parasites
- pH & turbidity

**Cost:** $150-$300 for full panel

**Test frequency:**
- New system: Once before use
- Ongoing: Annually (recommended)
- After problems: Immediately

---

## Protecting Your Well

### DO:
✅ Test annually (cheaper than treatment)
✅ Maintain septic system religiously (pump on schedule)
✅ Monitor water quality for changes
✅ Install a water treatment system if at risk
✅ Know your property gradient (well downhill = high risk)
✅ Keep septic system maintenance records

### DON'T:
❌ Assume 100 feet is always safe
❌ Ignore water quality changes
❌ Skip septic pumping "to save money"
❌ Flush anything that harms bacteria
❌ Use old septic system records (can fail unexpectedly)
❌ Wait for health symptoms to test (by then it's advanced)

## If Your Well Is Contaminated

**Immediate steps:**
1. Stop drinking/cooking with the water
2. Boil water for hygiene only (boiling doesn't remove chemicals)
3. Get the well tested to identify contaminant
4. Get the septic system inspected
5. Contact county health department

**Treatment options:**
- **Shock chlorination** ($200-$500) — kills bacteria, temporary fix
- **Filtration system** ($500-$2,000) — removes particles, some bacteria
- **Reverse osmosis** ($1,500-$3,000) — removes most contaminants
- **Well replacement** ($3,000-$8,000+) — last resort, moved to higher ground

**Septic fixes:**
- If system is failing: Repair or replace ($2,000-$15,000)
- If distance is too close: Can't be fixed without moving well or septic
- If neighbor's system is failing: Limited options, contact county

## Property-Level Considerations

**If buying property with both septic & well:**

1. **Get soil evaluation** — determines safe distance effectiveness
2. **Test the well** — before purchase
3. **Inspect septic** — age, condition, maintenance history
4. **Check property survey** — confirm distances
5. **Verify gradient** — well position relative to septic

**If distances are marginal (<100 feet):**
- Install water treatment system
- Plan for annual testing
- Maintain septic meticulously
- Budget for treatment if contamination occurs

## County-Specific Rules

Rules vary by county. Washington counties often have stricter rules than state minimums:

**Example:** King County requires:
- 100 feet from septic drain field to well
- 50 feet from tank to well
- But also considers soil type & gradient (may increase distances)

**Check your county's rules:** [County Requirements Tool](/tools/county-requirements)

## Bottom Line

**Your well and septic are connected.** A failing septic doesn't just affect your drains—it affects your drinking water.

**Protect both:**
✅ Maintain septic system on schedule
✅ Test well water annually
✅ Address septic problems immediately
✅ Install treatment if at risk
✅ Know your property gradient

**One is expensive to fix. Both broken? Unaffordable.**
