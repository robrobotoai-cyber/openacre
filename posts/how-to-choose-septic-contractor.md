# How to Choose a Septic Contractor: Red Flags & Best Practices

Not all septic contractors are equal. Choosing the wrong one can lead to poor repairs, wasted money, and recurring problems.

## Red Flags to Avoid

### 🚩 Recommends Annual Pumping
If they're pushing annual service, they're pushing unnecessary revenue. Average is every 3-5 years.

### 🚩 Pressure to Use Additives
Septic tank additives don't work and waste money. EPA and Washington State both say so.

### 🚩 No Written Estimates
Get everything in writing—scope, cost, timeline, warranty.

### 🚩 "Trust Us" Approach to Diagnosis
Require an inspection. Don't pump without knowing why.

### 🚩 Won't Provide References
Call previous customers. Ask about cost overruns and problem recurrence.

### 🚩 Lowest Bid by Far
Septic work is commoditized. If one bid is 50% cheaper, they're cutting corners.

---

## Qualities of a Good Contractor

✅ **Licensed & Insured**
- Check Washington State Department of Health licensure
- Verify liability insurance ($500K+ minimum)
- Ask for proof

✅ **Experience**
- 5+ years in business
- Specializes in septic (not general plumbing)
- Knows local soil & regulations

✅ **Written Estimates**
- Details scope of work
- Lists all costs
- Provides timeline
- Includes warranty terms

✅ **References**
- Provides 3+ customer references
- References are recent (last 2 years)
- Easy to reach (real phone numbers, not generic)

✅ **Clear Communication**
- Explains diagnosis in plain English
- Answers questions patiently
- Calls if work scope changes
- Provides updates during project

✅ **Fair Pricing**
- Competitive with local market
- Itemized invoice
- Transparent about labor vs. materials
- No surprise charges

## The Right Questions to Ask

1. **"Are you licensed by Washington Department of Health?"**
   - Non-negotiable. Verify this independently.

2. **"What's your diagnosis—why does this repair need to happen?"**
   - Should reference inspection findings, not just "it's old."

3. **"What's your warranty on this work?"**
   - Good contractors warranty their work (1-5 years typical).

4. **"Can I see an itemized quote?"**
   - Labor, materials, permits—everything separate.

5. **"How long will this take?"**
   - Reasonable timelines prevent disruption.

6. **"Who's responsible if something goes wrong?"**
   - Contractor should stand behind their work.

7. **"Can you provide references?"**
   - Call them. Ask about cost overruns, recurrence of problems.

## Comparing Quotes

Don't just look at price. Compare:

| Factor | Good Contractor | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| Scope | Detailed, references inspection | Vague, generic |
| Price | Market-rate, itemized | Lowest bid, no breakdown |
| Timeline | Realistic (accounts for weather, permits) | Unrealistic ("done in 2 days") |
| Warranty | 1-5 years | None, or "as-is" |
| Communication | Calls before changes | Silent until final bill |
| References | Real, responsive, positive | None provided |

## The Inspection Before Work Begins

A good contractor will:
1. Conduct on-site inspection (free or minimal fee)
2. Identify the exact problem (not guessing)
3. Explain findings in writing
4. Provide repair options (repair vs. replace, timeline, cost)
5. Let you decide without pressure

---

## Questions About Specific Repairs

### **For Drain Field Work**
- "Is this a repair or replacement?"
- "What's the soil condition?" (if replacement, it matters)
- "Will my system be offline during work?" (timeline)
- "What's the new field's expected lifespan?"

### **For Tank Pumping**
- "When was it last pumped?"
- "What's the sludge level?" (helps predict next pumping)
- "Any concerns noted during inspection?"

### **For Repairs**
- "Is this a bandaid fix or permanent?"
- "What's the likelihood of recurrence?"
- "What maintenance prevents this in the future?"

## Bottom Line

A **good septic contractor** will:
- Diagnose accurately (inspection-based, not guessing)
- Explain clearly (plain English, no pressure)
- Price fairly (competitive, itemized)
- Warranty their work (stands behind it)
- Communicate throughout (updates, no surprises)

A **bad contractor** will:
- Pressure you to overpump or use additives
- Quote from the truck without inspecting
- Disappear after invoicing
- Deny responsibility if problems recur
- Make "emergency" repairs without alternatives

**Trust your gut.** If something feels off, get another quote.

## Next Steps

4. Choose based on experience + communication, not just price
